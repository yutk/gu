
# 9.3 The Fermionic Sector

In the case of the Fermionic content we can at the classical level take fields $\bar{\nu}, \bar{\zeta}, \nu, \zeta$ on $Y$ to be four distinct fields when ultimately we will wish to integrate out the Dirac like operator to take a Berezinian 'integral' in the quantum theory.

For $\nu, \bar{\nu} \in \Omega^{0}(Y, \mathfrak{F})$ and $\zeta, \bar{\zeta} \in \Omega^{1}(Y, \mathfrak{F})$ we can begin with operators like:

$$
\not D_{\omega}^{F}\binom{\zeta}{\nu}_{\rho\left(\varepsilon^{-1}\right)}=\not D_{\omega} \chi_{\varepsilon^{-1}}=0
$$

where $\chi$ contains three generations of observed Fermions as well as LookingGlass matter, dark Spinorial Matter, Rarita-Schwinger matter and more while $\mathcal{D}$ subsumes the Dirac Operators, and the various subfields of $\omega$ accommodate the functionings of the CKM matrix, the Higgs-Like soft mass fields, the Yukawa couplings, Gauge Potentials and the like.

Let us compile the Bosonic and Fermionic variations of the Spinorial Lagrangian terms in a single term:

$$
\Upsilon^{F}=*\left(\begin{array}{c}
d_{A_{\omega}} \nu+* \circ d_{A_{\omega}} \zeta \\
\oplus \\
d_{A_{\omega}}^{*} \zeta \\
\oplus \\
\bar{\nu} \zeta+\bar{\zeta} \nu+\circ \bar{\zeta} \zeta
\end{array}\right)
$$

as a kind of 1-form on some SuperSpace-like structure over $\mathcal{A}$ :

$$
\Upsilon_{\omega}^{F} \in\left(\begin{array}{c}
\Omega^{d-1}(Y, \mathfrak{F}) \\
\oplus \\
\Omega^{d}(Y, \mathfrak{F}) \\
\oplus \\
\Omega^{d-1}(Y, \text { ad })
\end{array}\right)
$$

which can be combined with the variation of either a first or second order purely Bosonic Lagrangian so as to form:

$$
\Upsilon_{\omega}=\Upsilon_{\omega}^{B}+\Upsilon_{\omega}^{F}=0 \quad \text { or } \quad \mathcal{D} \omega^{*} \Upsilon_{\omega}^{B}=\Upsilon_{\omega}^{F}
$$

At this point, we wish to take this mixed spinorial-tensorial $\Upsilon_{\omega}$ and ask whether we are attempting to penalize this expression in our extremization because it is

actually the obstruction term for a cohomology theory. That is, we choose to view 'wedging' with $\Upsilon_{\omega}$ as the application of a zeroth order operator and ask if it possesses a non-trivial square root the in form of a first order differential operator $\delta^{\omega}$ so that:

$$
\Upsilon_{\omega}=0 \text { and } \sqrt{\Upsilon_{\omega}}=\delta^{\omega} \quad \leadsto \quad\left(\delta^{\omega}\right)^{2}=0 \quad \leadsto \quad \text { Cohomology }
$$

to get a Lagrangian Cohomology theory of at least two steps:

$$
\Upsilon_{\omega}=\left(\delta^{\omega}\right)^{2}=\delta_{2}^{\omega} \circ \delta_{1}^{\omega}=0
$$

of a geometrically meaningful cohomology complex.

The first step then for us is to examine what we mean by 'essentially different directions' of perturbation as regards the symmetry built into the problem. As we have endeavored to keep our metric theory gauge theoretic, let us first try to remove the uninteresting redundancy that is merely due to the gauge symmetry of the $\mathcal{H}$ action.

The effect of an infinitesimal gauge transformation $\gamma \in T_{\varepsilon} \mathcal{H}$ on a point $g=(\varepsilon, \varpi) \in \mathcal{G}=P_{\mathcal{H}}$ is given by:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \frac{d}{d s} g \cdot \tau_{A_{0}}(\exp (s \gamma))=\frac{d}{d s}(\varepsilon, \varpi) \cdot \tau_{A_{0}}(\exp (s \gamma)) \\

\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\delta_{1}^{\omega}=\binom{\delta_{1, a}^{\omega}}{\bar{\delta}_{1, b}^{\omega}}=\binom{d_{A_{\omega}}}{D L_{\varepsilon_{\omega}}}

# 11 Observed Field Content

One of the features that arises when doing away with the primary nature of space-time and replacing a single metric space with a tension between two separate but related spaces linked by metrics, is that we find ourselves in the novel situation where we must relate fields that are native to different spaces. The principal means of doing this is via the pullback operation. To interpret differential equations governing fields native to $Y$ back on $X$ means pulling back not only bundles but so-called Jets or Sprays of sections on $Y$. But, to begin with, we can simply analyze the zeroth order of the activity on $Y$ by pulling back bundles via the $2^{n}$ operation derived from making a metric observation of $Y$ by $X$.

To begin with, let us detach from the spaces $T_{x} X$ to general metric vector spaces $W$ in even dimensions to say a few words about direct sums and tensor products of defining and spin representations for $\operatorname{Spin}(W)$.

there is a simple rule for tensor products of defining representations and spinors whereby the tensor product

$$
W \otimes \mathscr{S}_{W}=\mathscr{S}_{W} \oplus \mathbb{R}_{W}

breaks into a piece representing the action of gamma matrices as spinor endomorphisms and a second piece giving the pure Rarita-Schwinger spin $3 / 2$ representation corresponding to the sum of the highest weights of the factors.

We note further, that spinor representations carry the property of the exponential in that they take in direct sums as input and return products of the spinors of the summands as output.

$$
W=U \oplus V \quad \rightsquigarrow \quad \mathcal{S}(W)=\mathcal{S}(U \oplus V)=\mathcal{S}(U) \otimes \mathcal{S}(V)
$$

Both of these are likely to be well known to physicists. Somewhat less familiar is that the Rarita-Schwinger representation has slightly odd behavior when applied to direct sums of vector spaces.
